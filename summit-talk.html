The Pattern

Over the last century, every category revolution
followed the same three-pillar pattern.

Revolution 🧠 Preferences ⚡ Technology 💰 Economics
Commercial Aviation1950s People wanted to travel faster Jet engines matured Airlines made it cheaper than ships
Personal Computing1980s People wanted control over information Microprocessors shrank Moore's Law made it affordable
Smartphones2007 People wanted the internet everywhere 3G/4G networks were ready Carriers shifted from minutes to data
Streaming2010s People wanted content on demand Broadband reached everywhere Subscription replaced per-unit purchase

When all three converge — a new category is born. Every time.

The Nokia Moment

Bottled water proved
people would pay for water.

$350 billion global industry.

But it sells hydration — a commodity.

Everyone drinks water. Nobody experiences it.

Nokia proved people wanted mobile phones. They sold 1 billion of them.

But every Nokia was the same. A commodity.

Bottled water is the Nokia of water.

The Analogy

Nokia sold more phones
than anyone in history.

Then Apple — a computer company — asked a different question.

Nokia's question How do we make a better phone?
Apple's question What if the phone was a platform for everything?
Industry asks How do we sell more water?
Fine water asks What if water was a platform for experience?

The iPhone was 3–5× more expensive than any phone. It outsold them all.

The Parallel

Apple flipped
the power dynamic.

Before Carriers controlled everything — which phones, which software, what you could do. The phone was a commodity controlled by the carrier.
After Experience mattered more than the network. The carrier became invisible.
Today Nestlé and Danone control the shelf. They decide what's on it. Water is a commodity controlled by volume producers.
The flip The experience — minerality, pairing, story — matters more than the volume. The craft producer becomes the brand.
